The Wrong Robot

Jiro's father was an engineer who liked making helpful things. One Sunday he made a small robot for the family. It had short metal legs, a round head, and a button which said HELP.

"Call it Robo-ji," said Father.

Robo-ji opened its eyes. "How nice!" it said. Then it picked up a plate of soup and put it on Jiro's math book.

"That is not helpful," said Jiro.

"Maybe the book was hungry," said Robo-ji.

Mother asked Robo-ji to clean the living room. Robo-ji picked up every shoe, bag, and newspaper. Then it put all of them in the bathtub.

"The bathtub was empty," said Robo-ji. "Now it is full of family."

Father opened his notebook. "I am not sure about the program."

Jiro felt bad for the robot. Robo-ji was not trying to make trouble. It was trying too hard. So Jiro wrote rules: ask before you move something; food for people is not always food for dogs; if you do not understand, say maybe.

That afternoon, Grandma brought a box of old cups. Robo-ji asked before moving each cup. After sixty questions, everyone was tired.

Jiro added Rule 4: Do not ask the same question sixty times.

At dinner, Robo-ji made sandwiches correctly. Pochi, the dog, looked sad, so Robo-ji gave him one more.

"Rule 5," said Mother. "Robots may be kind."
