The Wrong Robot

Sota's father was an engineer who liked making helpful things.
One Sunday he made a small robot with short metal legs, a round head, and a button that said HELP.

"Call it Robo-ji," said Father.

Robo-ji opened its eyes. "How nice!" it said.
Then it picked up a plate of soup and put it on Sota's math book.

"That is not helpful," said Sota.

"Maybe the book was hungry," said Robo-ji.

Mother asked Robo-ji to clean the living room.
Robo-ji picked up every shoe, bag, and newspaper and put them all in the bathtub.

"The bathtub was empty," it explained. "Now it is full of family."

Sota did not want to hurt Robo-ji, so he wrote three rules.
Ask before you move something.
Food for people is not always food for dogs.
If you do not understand, say maybe.

Robo-ji read the rules carefully.

When Grandma came with a box of old cups, Robo-ji asked before moving each cup.
After sixty questions, Sota wrote one more rule:
Do not ask the same question sixty times.

Robo-ji nodded.
"Maybe this is a good rule."
