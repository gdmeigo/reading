The Wrong Robot

Sota's father was an engineer who liked making helpful things. One Sunday he made a small robot for the family. It had short metal legs, a round head, and a button which said HELP.

"Call it Robo-ji," said Father.

Robo-ji opened its eyes. "How nice!" it said. Then it picked up a plate of soup and put it on Sota's math book.

"That is not helpful," said Sota.

"Maybe the book was hungry," said Robo-ji.

Mother laughed once, but only once. "Robo-ji, please clean the living room."

Robo-ji looked around quickly. It picked up every shoe, every bag, and every newspaper. Then it put all of them in the bathtub.

"Why?" asked Sota.

"The bathtub was empty," said Robo-ji. "Now it is full of family."

The next problem came before lunch. Robo-ji wanted to make bread. It understood bread and meat, hard and soft, crushing, tasting, and teeth. That sounded good. But it did not understand one thing: the difference between "for lunch" and "for the dog."

It made twelve sandwiches for Pochi, the dog, and one tiny sandwich for the family.

Pochi looked happy.

Father opened his notebook. "I am not sure about the program."

"I am," said Sota. "The program is wrong."

Robo-ji looked sad. "Do not be against Robo-ji."

Sota felt bad. The robot was not trying to make trouble. It was trying too hard. Sota sat next to it and wrote three rules.

Rule 1: Ask before you move something.

Rule 2: Food for people is not always food for dogs.

Rule 3: If you do not understand, say maybe.

Robo-ji read the rules. "Maybe I understand."

That afternoon, Grandma came to visit. She brought a box of old cups. "Please be careful," she said.

Robo-ji picked up one cup and asked, "May I move this?"

"Yes," said Grandma.

Robo-ji moved it onto the table. Then it picked up another cup. "May I move this?"

"Yes."

After twenty cups, Grandma sat down. After forty cups, Father hid in the kitchen. After sixty cups, Sota said, "Robo-ji, you can move the rest."

Robo-ji stopped. "Rule 1 says ask before you move something."

Sota thought for a moment. Then he wrote Rule 4: Do not ask the same question sixty times.

Robo-ji read it and nodded. "Maybe this is a good rule."

At dinner, Robo-ji made sandwiches again. This time there were four big sandwiches for the family and one small sandwich for Pochi.

Pochi looked against the plan.

Robo-ji gave him one more.

"Rule 5," said Mother. "Robots may be kind."

Robo-ji's eyes became bright. "How nice!" it said, and for once, nothing went into the bathtub.
