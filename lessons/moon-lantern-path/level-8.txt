The Moon Lantern Path

Yui found the first lantern under the old bridge.

It was small, white, and dry, though rain had fallen all afternoon. A silver moon was painted on one side. On the other side were three words:

LIGHT ME LAST.

Yui picked it up carefully. Haru looked at the dark river below.

"Why last?" he asked.

"Maybe there are more," Yui said.

There were.

The second lantern was hanging from a tree near the school gate. The third was on the stone steps by the shrine. The fourth was in front of the closed sweet shop.

Each lantern had a moon on it. Each one had a number. But the numbers were strange.

Four.
Two.
Five.
One.

"Where is three?" Haru asked.

Yui smiled. "That is the question the path wants us to ask."

At sunset, Mio and Haru joined them. Mio drew a map. Haru carried the lanterns and tried not to drop them.

"Why are we following lanterns?" Haru asked.

"Because they were left for someone," Yui said.

"Maybe they were left by the moon," Haru said.

Mio looked at the sky. "The moon is not up yet."

"Then it is very organized," Haru said.

They walked to the park. The missing third lantern was there, floating in the dry fountain. It was tied to a little wooden boat.

On the boat was a note:

WHEN FIVE ARE FOUND, ONE WISH CAN BE CARRIED.

The note was written in careful letters. Yui touched the paper. "This is not a game."

Haru looked at the lantern numbers again. "Maybe the order is one, two, three, four, five."

Mio shook her head. "Then why does the first one say light me last?"

They put the lanterns on the ground. One was from the sweet shop. Two was from the tree. Three was from the fountain. Four was from the bridge. Five was from the shrine steps.

Yui looked at the map. "If we connect the places in number order, what shape do we get?"

Mio drew lines. The lines made a crooked star.

At the center of the star was an empty lot.

The empty lot had once been a small garden. Now it had weeds, stones, and one old bench. The bench was wet, but a folded towel had been put on it.

On the towel was a photo of an old woman and a little girl. The old woman held a lantern. The little girl held a paper moon.

Yui knew the old woman. "This is Mrs. Mori from the sweet shop."

"The shop was closed last month," Haru said.

Haru read the back of the photo. "For the night when the path is forgotten."

Then the first lantern began to shine.

No one touched it. No match was used. No switch was found.

The light was soft and blue.

Mio stepped back. "That lantern was not lit by us."

The second lantern shone. Then the third. Then the fourth. Then the fifth.

The empty lot changed. Not completely. It was still a lot with weeds. But in the blue light, they could see a path of small white stones. The stones made the same crooked star.

At the center was a metal box.

Haru opened it. Inside were many folded papers. Each paper had a wish written on it.

Please let my shop be warm.
Please let my daughter come home.
Please let someone remember the moon path.

Yui read the last paper twice.

Mrs. Mori came slowly from the street. She carried a bag of sweets.

"I hoped someone would find them," she said.

"Were the lanterns put out by you?" Mio asked.

Mrs. Mori nodded. "They were made by my granddaughter and me. Long ago, children walked this path at the summer festival. We carried wishes to the garden. But the garden was sold, and the path was forgotten."

Yui looked at the old lot. "Can it be remembered again?"

Mrs. Mori smiled sadly. "I am too old to do it alone."

Haru raised his hand. "We are not too old."

The next week, the lot was cleaned by students and neighbors. Stones were washed. Weeds were pulled. The old bench was fixed by Haru and Mr. Sato. A new sign was written by Yui:

MOON LANTERN PATH.
BRING A SMALL WISH.

At the festival, people came with paper moons. Some wishes were serious. Some were funny. Haru wrote, "Please let homework become smaller." Mio wrote, "Please let Haru read directions."

Yui did not write a wish at first. She watched Mrs. Mori light the last lantern.

"Why last?" Haru asked softly.

Yui smiled. "Because the last light shows that the path is complete."

Then she wrote her wish:

Please let forgotten things find people.

The lanterns were carried by many hands. The moon came up over the bridge. The path was not forgotten that night.
