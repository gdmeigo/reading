The Moon Cup

Yui visited her grandmother's house during the summer festival. The house was old, but it was full of small bright things: glass bells, red paper birds, and cups which looked too thin to use.

"Do not drink from the blue cup after sunset," said Grandma.

That was the kind of sentence that made Yui want to understand more.

After dinner, Haru came to the house with festival tickets. "What's wrong?" he asked. Yui was standing in front of the blue cup. It was on a shelf between two white cups. It looked clear, but when Yui looked through it, the room was upside down.

"Maybe it is just old glass," said Haru.

"Maybe," said Yui. "But the moon in it is moving."

They waited until the house was quiet. Then Yui put a little water in the cup. The water did not go down. It rose like steam, but it was cold. A small moon appeared above the cup.

A voice said, "I am not sure about this house."

Haru almost dropped his tickets. "The cup can talk?"

"The moon can talk," said the voice. "The cup is only my door."

The moon was looking for its lost shadow. Without the shadow, it could not make the festival lanterns shine. Yui wanted to help, but Grandma's rule was clear: do not drink.

So Yui did not drink. She wrote a question on paper and held it over the cup: What do you need?

The answer appeared in silver letters: Bring back the thing that looks full but has nothing in it.

Haru and Yui looked through the house. A box? A bag? A bottle? Then Haru saw a festival drum. It was round like a full moon, but inside it was empty. On the back of the drum was a black circle.

"This is the shadow," said Yui.

The black circle did not want to move. It was stuck against the wood. Haru tried to pull it. Yui tried to push it. Nothing happened.

Then Grandma came in. "You two are noisy," she said. But she was smiling. "You found it."

Grandma beat the drum once. The black circle jumped out, crossed the room, and fell into the blue cup. The small moon became bright. Outside, every lantern in the street turned on at the same time.

Yui looked at Grandma. "Why did you say not to drink?"

"Because," said Grandma, "people who drink moon water talk in poems for three days."

Haru looked worried. "Did you drink it before?"

Grandma closed one eye. "Maybe."

At the festival, the lanterns were more beautiful than ever. Yui did not tell anyone about the moon. But when she saw the blue cup later, one silver word was inside it: Thanks.
